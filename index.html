What is a Research Design?

A research design is the comprehensive framework that dictates the entire flow of a research study. It serves as a strategic blueprint, providing a detailed plan of action before any data is collected. Its primary purpose is to structure the research process in a way that effectively addresses the central research questions and hypotheses logically and coherently. A well-crafted design maximizes the reliability and validity of the findings by ensuring the data collected is relevant and the analysis is objective, making it the essential bridge from question to conclusion.

Features of a Good Research Design

A quality design is characterized by several key features.

  • Generalizability: The findings should be applicable to a larger population.

What is Experimental Research Design?

The gold standard for establishing causal relationships.

The experimental design is a scientific method where a researcher manipulates one or more independent variables and applies them to one or more dependent variables to determine their influence. It is an attempt by the researcher to maintain control over all factors that may affect the result, allowing them to determine or predict what may occur.

This method relies on controlled methods, random assignment, and the manipulation of variables to test a hypothesis.

The Architect's Blueprint

Winer (1971) compared the design of an experiment to an architect‚Äôs plan... the researcher has to plan... so that the experiment on completion fulfils the objectives of research.

Just as a blueprint outlines the final structure of a building, an experimental design provides a detailed plan for the entire research process, ensuring objectives are met.

Core Principles

  • üî¨Sophisticated Method: Considered the most scientifically rigorous research method.
  • üîóCausal Relationships: Uniquely suited to examine hypothesized causal links between dependent and independent variables.
  • üéõÔ∏èControl: The researcher actively controls factors and variables to isolate the cause-and-effect relationship.
  • üîçObjectivity: Carried out in an objective and controlled fashion to maximize precision and draw specific conclusions.

"Experimental design is the process of carrying out research in an objective and controlled fashion so that precision is maximized and specific conclusions can be drawn regarding a hypothesis statement."

- Bell, 2009

"The design of an information-gathering experiment in which a variation is present or not, and it should be executed under the researcher's complete control."

Basic Terminology in Experimental Design

Diving deeper into the language of experiments.

üÖ∞Ô∏èFactors: The Independent Variables

Any independent variable used in an experiment, whether it's a treatment or a classification, is called a factor. Factors are typically denoted by capital letters like A, B, C, etc.

1. Treatment Variable (E-type Factor)

This is a factor that the experimenter actively manipulates.

Example: An experimenter administers different teaching methods to different groups to see the effect on achievement scores. The "teaching method" is the treatment variable.

2. Classification Variable (S-type Factor)

This is a factor based on a pre-existing characteristic of the subjects, which is manipulated through selection, not direct intervention.

Examples: Sex, age, IQ level, socioeconomic status, genetic errors, etc. These are characteristics the subjects already possess.

The Role of S-Type Factors

S-type factors are generally included to classify subjects for control purposes. However, sometimes the experimenter is interested in evaluating the effect of these classification factors themselves.

Example: An experimenter might classify subjects into low, medium, and high socioeconomic groups to assess differences in a dependent variable among these groups.

Putting It Together: A Two-Factor Experiment

Imagine an experiment designed to study the effects of both strain and rearing environment on children's performance. In this study:

  • Factor A: Strain (e.g., a classification or S-type factor).
  • Factor B: Rearing Environment (e.g., a treatment or E-type factor that could be manipulated).

üìäLevels

Each specific variation or category within a factor is called a level. A factor must have at least two levels to be compared.

Example: Factor 'Drug Dosage'

An experimenter wants to test the effect of a new drug. The factor is the 'drug', and the chosen levels could be different dosages:

0 mg (Placebo) 5 mg 10 mg

Notation

Levels are designated by a lowercase letter of the factor symbol with a subscript. If Factor is A, its levels are:

a‚ÇÅ, a‚ÇÇ, a‚ÇÉ, ..., a‚Çö

üé≤Dimensions

The dimension of an experiment describes its complexity, indicated by the number of factors and the number of levels within each factor.

Example: A 2x3x4 Factorial Design

An experiment with three factors (A, B, C):

  • Factor A: Gender (2 levels: Male, Female)
  • Factor B: Socio-economic Status (3 levels: Low, Middle, High)
  • Factor C: Age Group (4 levels: 15-20, 21-25, 26-30, 31-35)

The dimension of this experiment is written as 2 √ó 3 √ó 4.

General Form

A three-factor experiment with 'p' levels for Factor A, 'q' levels for Factor B, and 'r' levels for Factor C is designated as a p √ó q √ó r factorial experiment.

üß©Treatment Combinations

A treatment combination is a particular set of experimental conditions created by combining one level from each factor in the experiment.

Single-Factor vs. Multi-Factor

Though sometimes used interchangeably, 'treatment' and 'treatment combination' have a slight difference:

  • In a single-factor study, the levels of the factor are the treatments. If studying IQ's effect on achievement with levels 90, 100, and 110, you have 3 treatments.
  • In a multi-factor study (e.g., 2x3 design), you have 2 x 3 = 6 unique treatment combinations. Each combination is a specific pairing of a level from Factor A and a level from Factor B.

üîÅReplication

The term replication refers to an independent repetition of the experiment under conditions that are as identical as possible.

Key Principle

The experimental subjects used in each repetition must be independent samples that are randomly drawn from the population being studied. This helps ensure that the results are reliable and not due to chance or a specific group of subjects.

üìàMain Effects

The main effect of a factor is the difference in performance from one level to another for that specific factor, averaged across the levels of all other factors in the study. In statistical terms, the mean squares for the levels of a factor represent its main effect.

Example: A 2x3x4 Factorial Experiment

Consider an A √ó B √ó C experiment where Factor A has 2 levels (a‚ÇÅ, a‚ÇÇ), Factor B has 3 levels (b‚ÇÅ, b‚ÇÇ, b‚ÇÉ), and Factor C has 4 levels (c‚ÇÅ, c‚ÇÇ, c‚ÇÉ, c‚ÇÑ).

  • The main effect of Factor A is the difference in performance between level a‚ÇÅ and a‚ÇÇ, averaged across all levels of B and C.
  • The main effect of Factor B is the difference in performance between levels b‚ÇÅ, b‚ÇÇ, and b‚ÇÉ, averaged across all levels of A and C.
  • And so on for Factor C. Main effects can also be represented graphically to visualize their impact.

üîçSimple Effect

In a factorial experiment, the simple effect is the effect of one factor at a single, specific level of another factor. It's used to break down and understand interaction effects.

Example: A 2x2 Factorial Experiment

Imagine a study with two factors, A and B, each with two levels.

  • The simple effect of A would be the effect of A's two levels at the first level of B, and then separately at the second level of B.
  • Similarly, the simple effect of B is its effect at each of the two levels of A.

Real-world scenario: Analyzing the effect of social media (Factor A) on youths vs. adolescents (Factor B) within urban areas, and then separately within rural areas.

A method design that allows the researcher to test a hypothesis by drawing reliable conclusions regarding the links between independent and dependent variables.

1Ô∏è‚É£Pre-Experimental Designs

As the name suggests, pre-experimental research happens before the true experiment starts. This is done to determine the researchers‚Äô intervention on a group of people. This will help them tell if the investment of cost and time for conducting a true experiment is worthwhile. Hence, pre-experimental research is a preliminary step to justify the presence of the researcher‚Äôs intervention.

The pre-experimental approach helps give some sort of guarantee that the experiment can be a full-scale successful study.

The pre-experimental design does not have a comparison group. This means that while a researcher can claim that participants who received a certain treatment have experienced a change, they cannot conclude that the change was caused by the treatment itself.

Characteristics of Pre-experimental Designs

  • Generally uses only one group for treatment, which makes observation simple and easy.
  • Validates the experiment in the preliminary phase itself.
  • Pre-experimental design tells the researchers how their intervention will affect the whole study.
  • As they are conducted in the beginning, pre-experimental designs give evidence for or against their intervention.
  • It does not involve the randomization of the participants.
  • It generally does not involve a control group, but in some cases where there is a need for studying the control group against the treatment group, static-group comparison comes into the picture.
  • The pre-experimental design gives an idea about how the treatment is going to work in case of actual true experiments.

Types of Pre-Experimental Designs

Exploring the three main frameworks in this category.

1One-shot Case Study Design

This design practices the treatment of a single group and takes only a single measurement after the experiment. It analyzes post-test results by comparing them to the expected outcome, making it clear how the case would have looked without the treatment.

Key Features:

  • Treats a single group.
  • Takes a single measurement after the experiment.
  • Only analyzes post-test results.
  • No control group.

Example:

A team leader implements a new soft skills program. At the end of the first month, employees are measured for improvement. The leader knows the impact of the program on the employees.

2One-group Pretest-Posttest Design

Like the previous design, this also works on just one experimental group. However, it takes two measures into account: a pre-test and a post-test. The pre-test shows the group's state before treatment, and the post-test determines the changes after the treatment. While it sounds like a true experiment, it does not have a control group.

Key Features:

  • Works with a single group.
  • Includes both a pre-test and a post-test.
  • Measures changes within the same group.
  • No control group.

Example:

A team leader conducts a test before implementing the soft skills program to gauge employee skill levels. After the training, a second test is conducted to measure their status. This provides a clear frame of reference for the program's impact.

3Static-group Comparison

This design compares two groups. One group (the experimental group) is exposed to the treatment, while the other (the control group) is not. The difference between the two groups after the experiment is the result. Because it involves a control group, this design is sometimes considered a quasi-experimental design.

Key Features:

  • Compares two groups.
  • Includes an experimental group and a control group.
  • Determines the outcome by comparing both groups.

Example:

The team leader gives soft skills training to one group of employees, while another group remains a control group. After the training, he compares the two groups and finds the treated group shows more evolved soft skills than the control group.

Validity, Advantages, and Disadvantages

Validity of Results

In pre-experimental research, the validity of results is a tough catch. It is challenging to definitively prove that the treatment caused the observed change because there are no control groups for comparison. Without a comparison group, it's hard to tell how significant or valid a result is. The results may have occurred due to other factors like the group's natural maturation or just by chance.

Advantages of Pre-experimental Designs

  • Cost-effective: They are simple and quick to conduct, which makes them a low-cost option for initial research.
  • Simple to Conduct: The straightforward process makes this a good starting point for researchers.
  • Efficient in Natural Environments: This design is effective when studying groups in their natural, unaltered setting.
  • Suitable for Beginners: It is an ideal starting point for researchers who are new to experimental design.
  • Less Human Intervention: It requires minimal interference with the subjects, making the process less complex.
  • Preliminary Check: It helps determine if a true experiment is worth the investment of time and resources.

Disadvantages of Pre-experimental Designs

  • Weak Causal Relationships: It is not a strong design for determining cause-and-effect relationships between variables.
  • Lack of Control: Researchers have little to no control over the study, which can lead to biased results.
  • High Threat to Internal Validity: The absence of a control group and other factors makes it difficult to trust the results' integrity.
  • Results Are Unreliable: The lack of a comparison group makes the results less reliable and difficult to generalize.

2Ô∏è‚É£True-Experimental Designs

This is the most accurate form of experimental research design as it relies on the statistical hypothesis to prove or disprove a hypothesis. This is the most commonly used method implemented in Physical Science. True experimental research design is the only method that establishes the cause-and-effect relationship within the groups.

The factors that need to be satisfied in this method are:

  • Randomization: Random variable.
  • Manipulation: Variable can be manipulated by the researcher.
  • Control Groups: A group of participants is familiar with the experimental group, but the experimental rules do not apply to them.
  • Experimental Group: Research participants where experimental rules are applied.

It may be performed on at least two randomly assigned dependent subjects with or without a pretest. A true experimental research design must have a control group, a variable that can be modified by the researcher, and a random distribution.

Properties of True Experimental Design

üî®

Manipulation

The process of intentional change of testing variables, generally, the independent variables (treatment variables) of a study to see the effects on the dependent variables.

üîí

Control

In experimental research, control is the effort to remove the influence of extraneous variables on the dependent variable under investigation.

üé≤

Randomization

Randomization in experimental design is essentially the process of randomly assigning participants across experimental and control groups. It assumes that all participants are equally to be assigned to any condition without bias.

Process of Experimental Design

A step-by-step guide to conducting true experimental research.

1.

Define Research Problem

Identify the problem. Experimental research is primarily conducted to solve practical, rather than theoretical, problems.

2.

Determine Variables

A research question must be transformed into an experimental hypothesis by defining the key variables and predicting their relationships.

3.

Formulate Hypothesis

Create a precise, testable hypothesis that corresponds to the research topic.

4.

Design a Controlled Experiment

Create a plan that addresses all elements and conditions. This involves:

  • Manipulating the independent variable(s) systematically and precisely.
  • Measuring the dependent variable(s) with precision.
  • Controlling any factors that can cause confusion.

5.

Design Experimental Treatments

Manipulation of the independent variable can have an impact on the experiment's external validity or the extent to which the results can be extended and applied to the broader world. The first step is to consider how widely the independent variable should be varied. The second step is to decide how finely to alter the independent variable. If subjects are exposed to more than one treatment, then the findings could only be generalized to individuals exposed to the same treatments in the same order of presentation.

6.

Measure Dependent Variables

Finally, determining the collection method of data on the dependent variable outcomes. The aim should be to achieve accurate and valid measures that reduce research bias or inaccuracy. Some factors can be best measured using scientific tools. Others may need to be operationalized prior to being measured.

Blinding in Experimental Design

Preventing bias in clinical trials.

Blinding is a methodological technique used in clinical trials to prevent bias by keeping participants, investigators, and assessors unaware of treatment group assignments. It involves concealing research design elements like the group assignment, treatment agent, and research hypothesis. Blinding can help improve the accuracy of results by reducing error that may result from subjects' knowledge of their assignment to study conditions.

Qualities of Effective Blinding

  • Concealment: The technique must successfully hide the group allocation.
  • Assessment: The technique must not impair the ability to accurately assess outcomes.
  • Acceptance: The technique must be acceptable to the individuals who will be assessing outcomes.

Types of Blinding

  • Single-blind: Conceals from the participants whether a treatment or placebo is administered.
  • Double-blind: Conceals it from participants and administrators. In double-blind experiments, the group assignment is hidden from both the participant and the person administering the experiment.
  • Triple-blind: Researchers can also blind the individuals performing the statistical analysis by labeling the groups with no identifying terms, such as A and B.

Blinding helps ensure a study‚Äôs internal validity, or the extent to which you can be confident any link you find in your study is a true cause-and-effect relationship.

Double or triple blinding is often not possible. While medical experiments can usually use a placebo or fake treatment for blinding, in other types of research, the treatment sometimes cannot be disguised from either the participant or the experimenter. For example, many treatments that physical therapists perform cannot be faked.

Types of True Experimental Designs

1. Two Groups Post-Test Only

Subjects are randomly selected and assigned to experimental and control groups. A treatment or intervention is given to the experimental group. Afterward, both groups are tested and conclusions are drawn from the comparisons of both results.

Example:

To study the effects of Technology Integration on the Academic Performance of Students, no technology intervention is given to the control group while technology intervention is given to the experimental group. Types of technology lessons and frequency of technology lessons are used (Manipulation). After the intervention, if students of the control group score a B grade and the experimental group students score an A grade, it can be concluded that the technology intervention has a positive impact on the academic performance of students.

2. Two Groups Pre-Test and Post-Test

Subjects are randomly selected and assigned to an experimental and a control group. Both groups are tested before the intervention or treatment (pretest). A treatment or intervention is given to the experimental group. Both groups are then tested after the intervention (posttest) to determine the degree of changes in the groups.

Example:

To study the effect of role-playing as a teaching strategy on students‚Äô reading comprehension, a pretest is administered to both a control group and an experimental group. After the pretest, the control group is not given any new strategy and continues with regular teaching activities, while the experimental group is taught with different role-playing techniques. After the intervention, the investigator will see if there is a difference between the pretest and post-test scores. If pre-test scores are B in both groups and after the intervention post-test scores are B and A+ in the control group and experimental group respectively, it is concluded that role-playing as a teaching strategy has been effective for enhancing students' reading comprehension skills.

A two-group pre-test post-test design is an experimental design where a sample is randomly divided into two groups, and each group is measured before and after a treatment:

  • Random assignment: Subjects are randomly assigned to either a treatment group or a control group.
  • Pretest: All subjects complete a pretest before the treatment.
  • Treatment: The treatment group receives the treatment, while the control group does not.
  • Posttest: All subjects complete a posttest after the treatment.

This design is used to determine conclusions about a phenomenon or to test a hypothesis. The design is considered a mixed design because it has two independent variables: treatment, which is the between-groups variable, and change over time, which is the within-subjects variable.

The paired samples t-test, also known as the dependent samples t-test, is a statistical test that can be used to compare pre-test and post-test scores.

3. Solomon Four-Group Design

The Solomon four-group design is a research method developed by Richard Solomon in 1949. It is sometimes used in social science, psychology, and medicine. This design is highly robust as it can be used if there are concerns that the treatment might be sensitized by the pre-test. In addition to the usual two groups (treatment and control), it has a second pair of groups who do not receive a pre-intervention evaluation.

The four groups in the Solomon design:

Group Pre-intervention Intervention Post-intervention
1 Test Treatment Test
2 Test Control Test
3 No test Treatment Test
4 No test Control Test

The first two groups receive the evaluation test before and after the study, as in a normal two-group trial. The second two groups receive the evaluation only after the study. This design contains two extra control groups, which serve to reduce the influence of confounding variables and allow the researcher to test whether the pretest itself has an effect on the subjects.

Analysis:

The effectiveness of the treatment can be evaluated by comparisons between groups 1 and 3, and between groups 2 and 4. In addition, the effect of the pre-treatment evaluation can be calculated by comparing the control group who received the pre-treatment evaluation with those who did not (groups 2 and 4).

Whilst much more complex to set up and analyze, this design combats many of the internal validity issues that can plague research. It allows the researcher to exert complete control over the variables and allows the researcher to check that the pretest did not influence the results.

Case Study: Resilience of Adolescents in a Tribal Area

Rationale

Resilience prevents the emergence of stress-related mental health problems among adolescents. Adolescents in tribal areas of India are more prone to develop such problems.

Objectives

The primary objective was to determine the effect of combined life skills-based health empowerment intervention on the resilience of school-going adolescents in a tribal area. Secondary objectives were to determine the effect of the intervention on internal health locus of control and self-determination, and to compare the effect of the intervention on resilience between non-tribal and tribal adolescents.

Methods

Researchers conducted this quasi-experimental study using a Solomon four-group design among 742 adolescents in two schools in Purulia, West Bengal, India. Students in the pretested group were examined for resilience using the Child Youth Resilience Measurement scale. A life skills education-based health empowerment intervention was administered to students in the experimental group. Post-test data on resilience, self-determination, internal health locus of control, and pathological behavior was obtained 3 months after the completion of the intervention. A multi-level general linear mixed model was constructed to determine the effect of the intervention on resilience.

Results

Resilience was lower among tribal adolescents at baseline. The intervention significantly improved resilience [$Œ≤_{Adjusted} = 11.19 (95\% CI = 10.55, 11.83)$], with a greater increase for tribal adolescents [$Œ≤_{tribal-nontribal} = 1.53 (95\% CI = 0.03, 3.03)$]. The intervention also significantly improved internal health locus of control (marginal mean increment $1.38 \pm 0.05$), self-determination (marginal mean increment $3.71 \pm 0.09$), and reduced pathological behavior of the adolescents.

Conclusion

This study informed the current health policy that the existing life skills education-based program should be reviewed and modified to include generic life skills, and the life skills education-based program should be coupled with developmental interventions aimed at improving adult education and family climate for optimum effect on mental health and health behavior of adolescents.

Quasi-experimental designs are similar to true experiments, but they lack random assignment to experimental and control groups. Quasi-experimental designs have a comparison group that is similar to a control group except assignment to the comparison group is not determined by random assignment. The most basic of these quasi-experimental designs is the nonequivalent comparison groups design.

A quasi-experiment is an empirical interventional study used to estimate the causal impact of an intervention on a target population without random assignment.

Characteristics of Quasi-Experimental Designs

  • No Random Assignment: Participants are not randomly assigned to the experimental or control groups.
  • Comparison Group: They use a pre-existing group or a non-randomly assigned comparison group instead of a true control group.
  • Manipulation of Variables: The researcher still manipulates the independent variable, but without the control offered by randomization.
  • Higher External Validity: Because they are often conducted in real-world or natural settings, the findings can be more easily generalized to other contexts.

"Quasi" indicates "partial," "half," or "pseudo." As a result, quasi-experimental research resembles, but is not the same as, actual experimental research. In a true experimental design, the participants of the group are randomly assigned. So, every unit has an equal chance of getting into the experimental group. In a quasi-experimental design, the participants of the groups are not randomly assigned. So, the researcher cannot make a cause-and-effect conclusion.

Thus, it is not possible to assign the participants to the group. Because participants in quasi-experiments are not assigned at random, they are applied in cases where randomization is difficult or impossible. This is prevalent in educational research, as administrators are hesitant to allow students to be chosen at random for experimental samples. Time series, no corresponding control group, and counterbalanced designs are all instances of quasi-experimental research designs.
